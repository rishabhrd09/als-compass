How to Plan Power Backup for Home ICU?

Why is This Important?

This may seem like an overly cautious plan, but we are planning for the worst-case scenario. You might not face any problems throughout the year, but that one rainy day when your area experiences severe power outages - the panic it creates is overwhelming. Having more backup gives you more time and peace of mind.

Real Experience: Be Prepared

Last July, we faced a power outage of around 10 hours in our area. Unexpected long outages during monsoon season are common. Good power backup planning can help you stay calm during such emergencies.

Key Preparations

  • Plan for at least 10 hours of backup - This gives you enough buffer for extended outages
  • Save contact numbers of people who can arrange generators immediately - Know who to call when UPS backup runs low
  • Keep electricity board contact numbers handy - For outage updates and priority restoration
  • Pre-inform the electricity department about your medical condition - Many electricity boards have provisions for priority power restoration for homes with life-support equipment

Understanding Power Backup

Your power backup system consists of three key components: batteries that store energy (measured in Amp-hours), devices that consume energy (measured in Watts), and the UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply) that converts and delivers stored energy to your equipment.

Backup Time Formula:
Backup Time = (Battery Capacity in Wh √ó 0.75) √∑ Total Load in Watts

The 75% efficiency accounts for battery and UPS energy losses.

What Does "25% Usage" Mean?

This is an important concept for planning backup time accurately. Consider this example: You need 10 hours of backup during a power cut.

The oxygen concentrator doesn't run continuously - perhaps it runs for 2.5 hours out of those 10 hours. That's 2.5 √∑ 10 = 25% duty cycle.

Planning Your Backup

Based on your power requirements, you have two options:

  • Option A: Have a backup UPS + battery set (Recommended)
  • Option B: Have an additional battery set with higher Ah capacity

Why Two Complete UPS + Battery Sets?

UPS devices are generally reliable, but during severe power outages with frequent fluctuations, they can sometimes develop problems. A sudden voltage spike or prolonged stress can cause internal faults. Having a second complete UPS + battery set ensures you have a fully functional backup if your primary UPS fails - not just extra battery capacity, but a complete working system ready to take over.

Why emergency mode matters: If one UPS runs out, the ventilator on backup UPS keeps running ‚Üí buying you critical time to arrange a generator, contact an electrician, or get help.

What Are the Key Tips for Power Backup?

  • Keep a second UPS as backup, or use an Inverter for secondary backup ‚Üí In case the primary UPS fails
  • Test your backup monthly ‚Üí Batteries degrade over time
  • Know your electrician's number ‚Üí For quick repairs
  • Label all plugs clearly ‚Üí Know which device is on which UPS
  • Keep generator fuel stocked ‚Üí You can't buy fuel during a power cut
  • Charge batteries fully ‚Üí Partial charge = reduced backup time

What Are the Warning Signs to Watch?

Warning Sign What It Means
UPS beeping continuously Battery low or overloaded
Backup time decreasing Batteries aging, may need replacement
Devices shutting off UPS overloaded or battery dead
UPS not switching on Internal fault, use backup UPS immediately

Important: If your UPS shows any warning signs, have it checked immediately. Don't wait for a power outage to discover problems.

How Do I Calculate Battery Capacity Needed?

Step 1: Calculate Total Energy Needed

Energy (Wh) = Load (Watts) √ó Backup Time (Hours) √∑ 0.75

Example: For 292W load for 8 hours: 292 √ó 8 √∑ 0.75 = 3,115 Wh

Step 2: Choose Battery Configuration

For a 72V UPS system with 12V batteries (6 batteries in series):

Battery Ah needed = Energy (Wh) √∑ 72V = 3,115 √∑ 72 = 43.3 Ah

So you would need 6 batteries of at least 45Ah each.

Our Actual Setup

Each UPS has: 6 √ó 42Ah √ó 12V = 3,024 Wh storage.
After 75% efficiency: 2,268 Wh usable energy.
At 292W average load: 2,268 √∑ 292 = ~7.8 hours backup.
