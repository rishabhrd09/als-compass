Respiratory Emergency Protocol

Practical Guide for Home Ventilator Care

Core Principle: Involve PALS in Every Decision

With time and experience, PALS understands their body better than anyone. They will learn to recognize patterns and guide you on what works for them.

Over time, PALS can tell you:

  • "I think I need Ambu now"
  • "This feels like deep suction is needed"
  • "Cough is stuck near balloon area"
  • "Pressure feels insufficient"
  • "Check ventilator - something feels off"
  • "I need neb, secretions feel thick"

Always ask PALS:

  • How are you feeling right now?
  • Is ventilator pressure feeling OK?
  • Any cough stuck? Where - chest or balloon area?
  • Feeling dry cough or wet secretions?
  • Balloon feeling too tight or too loose?
  • Any other discomfort?

Key Insight: The more you involve PALS in daily decisions, the more they understand what helps them. Eventually, PALS becomes your best guide in emergencies.

What We Monitor

When PALS shows discomfort, check these parameters:

SpO2 Pulse / HR Blood Pressure Tidal Volume (TV) Respiratory Rate (RR) Ventilator Graph Leak Values

Changes in these parameters indicate something is wrong. But always combine parameter reading with PALS feedback - they often sense discomfort before numbers change significantly.

Two-Person Approach

Person 1: Immediate Care

  • Start Ambu if needed
  • Talk to PALS, get feedback
  • Perform suction/neb

Person 2: Support

  • Check equipment & connections
  • Prepare supplies
  • Monitor parameters

Understanding Each Situation

Situation A: SpO2 Dropping / Low Tidal Volume

This indicates airway or secretion issue. PALS may feel breathless or struggling.

Sequence:

1Give Ambu immediately - provides relief

2Ask PALS - what are you feeling?

3Based on feedback, choose path

4NS + Ambu + Suction (skip NS if uncomfortable)

5Subglottic port suction

6Repeat until PALS says they feel better

Path Selection:

  • Wet/gurgling → NS + Ambu + Suction
  • Dry cough → Nebulization first, then suction
  • Stuck at balloon → Subglottic suction first (see warning)
  • Catheter blocked → Aggressive Ambu, consider TT change

Situation B: High Pulse / High BP with Normal SpO2

Important: If PALS is not a BP patient (no history of hypertension), then high BP/pulse usually means breathing discomfort. The body is working hard or PALS is uncomfortable.

Common causes:

  • Fever/Infection → Check temperature, get CBC
  • Stuck secretions → Clearance cycle will help
  • Discomfort → PALS needs repositioning or pressure adjustment

What to do:

  1. Check temperature
  2. Ask PALS about discomfort
  3. Give Ambu for 1 minute (quick relief)
  4. Do clearance cycle: Ambu → Suction → Neb → Ambu → Suction
  5. If pulse/BP settles → secretions were the cause
  6. If still high → get CBC, consult doctor

Situation C: PALS Says "Pressure Feels Insufficient"

When PALS reports ventilator pressure feeling low or inadequate:

Check Ventilator:

  • Settings unchanged?
  • Circuit properly connected?
  • Leak values normal?
  • Exhalation valve working?

Quick Test:

  • Give Ambu temporarily
  • If PALS feels better with Ambu → ventilator issue
  • Continue Ambu support
  • Call technician

Critical Warnings

⚠️ Balloon Cuff Area Secretions

This is the most delicate procedure. Only attempt if you have been trained.

Steps:

  1. FIRST: Do subglottic port suction (clear above-cuff secretions)
  2. If confident and trained:
    • Deflate cuff slowly
    • Quick suction
    • Reinflate immediately
  3. If not confident: Do subglottic suction only, call trained person

Why risky: Deflating the balloon can allow secretions to enter the lungs. That's why subglottic suction comes first, and the procedure must be quick.

⚠️ Mucus Plug (Catheter Completely Blocked)

If suction catheter won't pass at all:

  1. Give 2ml NS
  2. 8-10 firm Ambu squeezes
  3. Try catheter again
  4. If still blocked → Consider TT tube change
    • Use backup tube
    • Call trained person if needed
    • Don't delay - airway is compromised

Quick Reference

Equipment Checklist

Always Ready:

  • Ambu bag
  • Suction machine
  • Suction catheters
  • Normal saline (NS)
  • Syringes (5ml, 10ml)

Backup:

  • Spare TT tube (same size)
  • Nebulizer + medicines
  • Oxygen concentrator
  • Backup ventilator (if available)
